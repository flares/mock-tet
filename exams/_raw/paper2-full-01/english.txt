Q1 Read the following passage and answer Q1–Q8: "The library was Meena's favourite place in the whole school. Every afternoon, she would slip inside, breathe in the smell of old paper, and lose herself in stories. She read about explorers who crossed frozen tundras, scientists who stayed awake for weeks in pursuit of discovery, and ordinary children who found extraordinary courage. Her teachers noticed that her vocabulary grew richer each term. By the end of the year, she could hold her own in class discussions that once made her shrink into silence. The library, she later wrote, had given her a second world to live in." What is the main idea of this passage?
A) Libraries are important school infrastructure
B) Meena's vocabulary improved after reading science books
C) Reading widely helped Meena grow in language and confidence *
D) Teachers noticed Meena was absent from class every afternoon
E The passage as a whole shows how extensive reading built Meena's vocabulary, confidence, and participation — making option C the central idea.

Q2 Based on the passage in Q1, the phrase "a second world to live in" most likely means:
A) Meena wanted to leave her school
B) Books provided Meena with an imaginative and enriching experience *
C) Meena believed libraries were better than classrooms
D) She escaped from her family duties by reading
E "A second world" is a metaphor for the imaginative realm that reading opens up, extending one's experience beyond everyday life.

Q3 In the passage (Q1), what evidence shows that Meena's reading had a practical academic benefit?
A) She smelled the old paper in the library
B) She read about explorers and scientists
C) Her vocabulary grew richer and she participated more in discussions *
D) She stayed in the library every afternoon
E The passage explicitly states that her vocabulary grew and she could hold her own in class discussions — concrete academic benefits of her reading habit.

Q4 The word "extraordinary" in the passage (Q1) is closest in meaning to:
A) Ordinary
B) Unexpected and remarkable *
C) Frightening
D) Familiar and comfortable
E "Extraordinary" means beyond what is ordinary or usual — remarkable or exceptional — which is the opposite of the word "ordinary" used just before it in the sentence.

Q5 Which literary device is used in the phrase "breathe in the smell of old paper" in the passage (Q1)?
A) Simile
B) Hyperbole
C) Personification
D) Imagery *
E Imagery appeals to the senses — here, the sense of smell — to create a vivid mental picture. This is not a simile (no like/as), not personification (paper is not given human traits), and not hyperbole (no exaggeration).

Q6 The tone of the passage (Q1) can best be described as:
A) Critical and judgmental
B) Humorous and sarcastic

D) Anxious and uncertain
E The narrator describes Meena's library experiences with evident affection and admiration for how reading transformed her, establishing a warm, positive tone throughout.

Q7 Which inference is best supported by the passage (Q1)?

Q8 The passage (Q1) is best classified as what type of writing?
A) Discursive essay presenting two sides of an argument
B) Narrative prose describing a character's experience *

Q9 Read the following passage and answer Q9–Q15: "English is spoken as a second language by millions of children across India. In many Telugu-medium schools, students encounter English only inside the classroom, with little exposure outside. Research suggests that comprehensible input — language that is slightly above the learner's current level — is crucial for acquisition. Teachers who use only textbook drills may produce students who can fill in blanks but cannot construct meaning. Effective teachers create rich language environments: they read aloud, use stories, encourage peer talk, and allow students to draw on their home language when needed. Assessment, too, must move beyond grammar tests to include observation and portfolio evidence of real language use." According to the passage, what does the term 'comprehensible input' refer to?
A) Input that is far above the learner's ability to challenge them
B) Input delivered only through textbooks
C) Language input slightly above the learner's current level *
D) Grammar exercises that fill in blanks
E The passage directly defines comprehensible input as "language that is slightly above the learner's current level," which aligns with Krashen's i+1 formula.

Q10 According to the passage in Q9, what is a limitation of relying solely on textbook drills?
A) They are too expensive for Telugu-medium schools
B) They produce students who cannot construct meaning *
C) They ignore listening and speaking entirely
D) They are not aligned with the national curriculum
E The passage explicitly states that teachers who use only textbook drills "may produce students who can fill in blanks but cannot construct meaning" — showing the limitation of drill-only instruction.

Q11 Which of the following teaching strategies is NOT mentioned in the passage (Q9) as part of a rich language environment?

E The passage lists reading aloud, using stories, peer talk, and using L1 as strategies. Memorising grammar rules is the approach associated with the Grammar-Translation method and is not mentioned as a strategy for a rich language environment.

Q12 Based on the passage (Q9), which form of assessment does the author prefer?
A) Standardised grammar tests administered every week
B) Observation and portfolio evidence of real language use *
C) Fill-in-the-blank exercises from the textbook
D) Oral examinations conducted in English only
E The passage explicitly contrasts grammar tests with "observation and portfolio evidence of real language use," arguing for the latter — a formative, authentic approach.

Q13 The passage (Q9) implies that in many Telugu-medium schools, students:
A) Are fluent in English because they study it daily
B) Have rich English exposure from television and family
C) Encounter English mainly within the classroom *
D) Prefer English over Telugu for daily communication
E The passage directly states that in many Telugu-medium schools, "students encounter English only inside the classroom, with little exposure outside" — meaning classroom is their primary English environment.

Q14 What is the author's overall stance in the passage (Q9)?
A) English teaching in India is already highly effective
B) Rote methods are sufficient for functional English skills
C) Grammar-Translation is the most efficient method for Indian classrooms
D) Teaching must go beyond drills to create meaningful language experiences *
E Throughout the passage the author contrasts shallow drill methods with richer approaches (stories, peer talk, authentic assessment), clearly arguing for the latter stance.

Q15 The word "exposure" in the passage (Q9) is used to mean:
A) Revealing secrets to other students
B) The risk of catching a disease
C) Contact with and experience of the language *
D) Outdoor activities beyond the classroom
E In language learning contexts, "exposure" means contact with the target language in use — hearing it, reading it, encountering it in authentic situations.

Q16 According to Stephen Krashen's Input Hypothesis, language acquisition is best promoted when learners receive input that is:
A) Grammatically simple and fully within their current competence
B) Slightly above their current level of proficiency (i+1) *
C) Delivered exclusively through written texts
D) Based on explicit grammar rules taught before reading
E Krashen's i+1 formula holds that acquisition happens when input is comprehensible but just beyond the learner's current ability — challenging without being incomprehensible.

Q17 What is the key distinction between 'language acquisition' and 'language learning' as described by Krashen?
A) Acquisition is formal; learning is informal
B) Acquisition occurs subconsciously through meaningful use; learning is conscious study of rules *
C) Learning is faster than acquisition for young children
D) Acquisition depends on grammar drills; learning depends on conversation
E Krashen distinguishes acquisition (natural, subconscious, like a child picking up L1) from learning (deliberate, conscious study of language rules). Only acquisition produces fluent, spontaneous use.

Q18 In the Communicative Language Teaching (CLT) approach, what is the primary goal of classroom activities?
A) Accurate reproduction of grammar patterns
B) Memorisation of vocabulary lists
C) Meaningful communication and real-language use *
D) Translating passages from L1 to English
E CLT prioritises the ability to communicate meaningfully in authentic contexts over the ability to reproduce grammatical rules perfectly. Accuracy is pursued in service of communication, not as an end in itself.

Q19 A teacher asks students to role-play ordering food at a restaurant in English. This activity best reflects which method?
A) Grammar-Translation Method
B) Audio-Lingual Method
C) Communicative Language Teaching *
D) Direct Drilling Method
E Role-plays simulate real-life communication tasks, which is the hallmark of CLT. The focus is on authentic, meaningful interaction rather than pattern drilling or translation.

Q20 The Audio-Lingual Method (ALM) relies heavily on which learning theory?
A) Constructivism
B) Behaviourism — stimulus–response habit formation *
C) Nativist / Universal Grammar theory
D) Socio-cultural theory
E ALM is grounded in behaviourism: language is a set of habits formed through repetition, drilling, and reinforcement. Errors are corrected immediately to prevent "bad habits."

Q21 Which of the following best describes the role of a learner's home language (L1) in second-language acquisition?
A) L1 always interferes with L2 and should be completely banned from the classroom
B) L1 is irrelevant to the process of learning L2
C) L1 can serve as a cognitive resource and scaffolding tool when used judiciously *
D) L1 should replace L2 instruction at lower primary level
E Research supports the view that L1 provides a cognitive scaffold — learners translate concepts into the new language and build bridges. Banning L1 entirely can raise anxiety and hinder understanding, particularly for beginners.

Q22 In LSRW skill development, which skill is typically developed first in natural first-language acquisition?
A) Reading
B) Writing
C) Speaking
D) Listening *
E In natural L1 acquisition, children listen extensively before they produce spoken language. Listening (receptive skill) precedes speaking (productive), which precedes reading and writing.

Q23 A teacher uses a portfolio to assess her students' English progress. Which type of assessment does this represent?
A) Summative assessment
B) Norm-referenced assessment
C) Formative assessment *
D) Diagnostic placement test
E Portfolios collect ongoing evidence of learning over time, providing feedback to guide further learning — the defining feature of formative assessment. They are not used to rank students or assign final grades.

Q24 Which approach to grammar teaching is most consistent with the constructivist view of language learning?
A) Teaching grammar rules first, then applying them to exercises
B) Helping learners notice patterns in authentic texts and construct rules inductively *
C) Memorising a list of rules before any reading or writing
D) Correcting every grammatical error immediately during oral work
E Constructivism holds that learners build understanding by discovering patterns themselves. Inductive grammar teaching — noticing patterns in authentic texts — aligns with this view far better than deductive rule-memorisation.

Q25 What does 'process writing' emphasise compared to traditional composition tasks?
A) Producing a perfect first draft quickly
B) Neatness and handwriting quality
C) Multiple stages of drafting, peer review, and revision *
D) Memorising paragraph structures before writing
E Process writing treats writing as a recursive activity: brainstorming, drafting, getting feedback, revising, and editing. It emphasises the act of composing over a final polished product.

Q26 A student consistently confuses "was" and "were" in writing. The most appropriate first step for the teacher is:
A) Assign extra grammar drills on the same day
B) Deduct marks for every error in the next assignment
C) Diagnose whether the error is developmental or due to L1 interference *
D) Ask the student to copy correct sentences fifty times
E Effective remedial teaching begins with diagnosis — understanding why the error occurs (developmental stage, L1 Telugu syntax, or a rule misconception) before prescribing the right intervention.

Q27 In a multilingual classroom where students speak Telugu at home, which strategy best supports English language development?
A) Forbidding Telugu in the classroom at all times to force English use
B) Using Telugu only for classroom management and English for all content
C) Translating every English sentence into Telugu for students
D) Allowing strategic code-switching while gradually expanding English use *
E Research supports allowing learners to use their L1 as a bridge while gradually increasing L2 use. Strategic code-switching reduces anxiety and supports comprehension without abandoning the English language goal.

Q28 'Decoding' in the context of reading instruction refers to:
A) Summarising the meaning of a paragraph
B) Identifying the author's purpose
C) Converting written symbols (letters/words) into sounds or spoken language *
D) Making inferences about unstated information
E Decoding is the foundational reading skill of recognising the relationship between letters/graphemes and their sounds/phonemes, enabling a reader to pronounce written words. It is distinct from comprehension.

Q29 A teacher reads a story aloud with expression and pauses to ask prediction questions. What primary language skill is being developed in students?
A) Writing fluency
B) Decoding and phonics
C) Listening comprehension and inferencing *
D) Grammar pattern recognition
E Read-aloud with interactive questions targets listening comprehension — students must follow the narrative, interpret expression, and make inferences — a critical component of LSRW-based language development.

Q30 Which statement about the Direct Method of language teaching is accurate?
A) It relies on translation into the mother tongue to explain meaning
B) It teaches grammar rules explicitly before introducing vocabulary
C) It uses only the target language in the classroom and teaches meaning through demonstration *
D) It is identical to the Communicative Language Teaching approach
E The Direct Method prohibits use of L1 and relies on demonstration, objects, pictures, and actions to convey meaning entirely in the target language. It differs from CLT, which allows L1 and focuses on communicative function over form.
