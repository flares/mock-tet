Q1 Which principle of development states that children gain control of the head before the legs?
A) Proximodistal principle
B) Cephalocaudal principle *
C) Principle of uniformity
D) Principle of discontinuity
E Cephalocaudal development proceeds from head to toe, so head and neck control appear before leg control.

Q2 In Piaget's theory, conservation of number generally develops during which stage?
A) Sensorimotor
B) Pre-operational
C) Concrete operational *
D) Formal operational
E Concrete operational children can conserve number, mass, and volume when they can reason about concrete transformations.

Q3 Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development refers to the gap between:
A) inherited ability and classroom marks
B) what a learner can do alone and with guidance *
C) school curriculum and home language
D) physical age and mental age
E ZPD describes tasks a learner cannot yet do independently but can complete with support from a more knowledgeable person.

Q4 A teacher giving hints and gradually withdrawing help is using:
A) reinforcement
B) scaffolding *
C) punishment
D) maturation
E Scaffolding is temporary support that is reduced as the learner becomes more competent.

Q5 Kohlberg's conventional level of moral development is mainly guided by:
A) obedience to avoid punishment only
B) social approval and law-and-order thinking *
C) universal ethical principles
D) reflexive biological needs
E At the conventional level, children judge actions by social expectations, approval, rules, and authority.

Q6 Gardner's theory helps teachers understand that intelligence is:
A) a single fixed IQ score
B) limited to linguistic ability
C) expressed in multiple forms *
D) unrelated to learning
E Multiple intelligences include linguistic, logical, spatial, bodily, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, and naturalistic abilities.

Q7 Erikson's stage most associated with adolescence is:
A) trust versus mistrust
B) initiative versus guilt
C) identity versus role confusion *
D) integrity versus despair
E Adolescents explore values, roles, and self-concept, making identity formation the key psychosocial task.

Q8 The strongest example of gender stereotyping in a classroom is:
A) asking all students to clean their desks
B) assigning leadership roles only to boys *
C) forming mixed-ability groups
D) using examples from daily life
E Giving leadership only to boys reinforces a social belief that leadership is male, which is a gender stereotype.

Q9 A learner with dyscalculia is most likely to need support in:
A) reading musical notation
B) understanding numbers and arithmetic *
C) hearing classroom instructions
D) controlling eye movement
E Dyscalculia is a specific learning difficulty that affects number sense, calculation, and mathematical reasoning.

Q10 Universal Design for Learning mainly asks teachers to provide:
A) identical worksheets for all learners
B) multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression *
C) only oral tests
D) separate classrooms for children with disabilities
E UDL reduces barriers by offering varied ways to access content, stay engaged, and show learning.

Q11 In an inclusive classroom, the teacher should first:
A) lower expectations for children with disabilities
B) identify barriers and adapt teaching strategies *
C) exclude difficult topics
D) ask parents to teach the child at home
E Inclusion keeps high expectations while changing supports, materials, and assessment methods to remove barriers.

Q12 A child who learns by observing a peer solve a problem illustrates:
A) Bandura's social learning theory *
B) Pavlov's classical conditioning
C) Spearman's g factor
D) Kohlberg's moral dilemma
E Bandura emphasized observational learning, modelling, imitation, and vicarious reinforcement.

Q13 In Skinner's operant conditioning, a reward given after a desired response is:
A) negative punishment
B) positive reinforcement *
C) extinction
D) spontaneous recovery
E Positive reinforcement adds a pleasant consequence to increase the likelihood of the behavior.

Q14 Intrinsic motivation is best shown when a student studies because:
A) the teacher promises a prize
B) parents demand high marks
C) the topic is personally interesting *
D) a punishment is expected
E Intrinsic motivation comes from curiosity, interest, satisfaction, or a sense of competence.

Q15 Formative assessment is primarily used to:
A) rank students at the end of the year
B) support learning during instruction *
C) replace teaching
D) certify school completion
E Formative assessment provides feedback during learning so teaching and learning strategies can be adjusted.

Q16 A child-centered classroom is characterized by:
A) strict lecturing for the whole period
B) active participation and learner choice *
C) memorisation without discussion
D) punishment for wrong answers
E Child-centered teaching values active exploration, participation, and learning experiences connected to the learner.

Q17 Assimilation in Piaget's theory means:
A) changing an existing schema to fit new information
B) fitting new information into an existing schema *
C) copying adult behavior
D) forgetting unused knowledge
E Assimilation occurs when a child interprets a new experience using an existing mental structure.

Q18 Accommodation occurs when a learner:
A) changes a schema after new evidence *
B) repeats a memorised answer
C) receives a reward
D) avoids a task
E Accommodation modifies existing mental structures when current schemas do not explain new experiences.

Q19 The Right to Education Act 2009 emphasizes education for children aged:
A) 3 to 6 years
B) 6 to 14 years *
C) 14 to 18 years
D) 18 to 21 years
E The RTE Act makes free and compulsory education a right for children from 6 to 14 years of age.

Q20 A diagnostic test in learning is mainly used to:
A) award certificates
B) locate specific learning difficulties *
C) compare schools
D) select only gifted learners
E Diagnostic assessment identifies the nature and causes of learning problems so remediation can be planned.

Q21 A teacher encourages students to give more than one solution to a problem. This promotes:
A) rote learning
B) divergent thinking *
C) fixed response training
D) mechanical repetition
E Divergent thinking involves generating multiple possible solutions or ideas.

Q22 In Maslow's hierarchy, which need must be addressed before a hungry child can focus well on learning?
A) self-actualisation
B) aesthetic need
C) physiological need *
D) prestige need
E Physiological needs such as food, water, and rest are basic needs that strongly affect attention and learning.

Q23 Which is a constructivist teaching practice?
A) giving definitions for copying only
B) letting students build concepts through activities *
C) punishing every incorrect answer
D) asking only recall questions
E Constructivism views learners as active makers of meaning through experience, discussion, and reflection.

Q24 A portfolio is useful because it:
A) records only the final exam score
B) shows growth through collected work over time *
C) eliminates teacher feedback
D) compares one child with the whole class
E Portfolios document progress, effort, feedback, and reflection across a period of learning.

Q25 A child with ADHD may especially require:
A) long silent copying tasks only
B) clear routines, short tasks, and movement breaks *
C) exclusion from group work
D) removal from all academic tasks
E ADHD support often includes structured routines, brief instructions, active tasks, and positive reinforcement.

Q26 NCF 2005 strongly recommends:
A) learning without understanding
B) connecting knowledge to life outside school *
C) banning local examples
D) replacing assessment with punishment
E NCF 2005 emphasizes constructivist learning, reducing rote load, and linking school knowledge with life.

Q27 NEP 2020 highlights which approach for foundational and school learning?
A) rigid separation of subjects
B) multilingualism and experiential learning *
C) only annual board exams
D) no vocational exposure
E NEP 2020 values mother tongue, experiential learning, flexibility, skills, and competency-based education.

Q28 Emotional intelligence includes the ability to:
A) solve only algebraic problems
B) recognize and manage emotions *
C) memorise historical dates
D) ignore social relationships
E Emotional intelligence involves awareness, regulation, empathy, motivation, and social skills.

Q29 Individual differences mean that teachers should:
A) teach every child in exactly one way
B) use varied strategies and flexible assessment *
C) ignore learners who are slow
D) give only competitive tasks
E Learners differ in readiness, interests, pace, background, and learning preferences, so teaching must be flexible.

Q30 Peer influence during adolescence is important because adolescents:
A) stop needing social approval
B) seek identity and belonging *
C) cannot think abstractly at all
D) have no emotional changes
E Adolescents often explore identity and social belonging through peer relationships.
